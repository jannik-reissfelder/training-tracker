# Set-Volume Dose–Response for Hypertrophy and Strength

## Is 1–2 hard sets per exercise, full body 2–3 times per week, scientifically defensible for an intermediate lifter?

**Evidence review date:** July 2026

**Population of interest:** healthy, intermediate adult lifter

**Primary goal:** hypertrophy, while retaining time and recovery capacity for strength, movement quality, power, conditioning, and general fitness

---

## Executive summary

**Short answer:** Yes—**1–2 hard sets per exercise in a full-body routine performed 2–3 times per week is a scientifically supportable default**, especially when efficiency, adherence, and broader physical fitness matter. It is best described as a **minimum-effective to lower-moderate-volume strategy**, not as the most evidence-supported way to maximize hypertrophy.

The evidence does **not** support the stronger claim that 1–2 sets are generally *optimal* or equivalent to higher volumes for muscle growth. Across meta-analyses, hypertrophy tends to increase as weekly hard-set volume increases, with diminishing returns rather than a sharp universal threshold. The newest peer-reviewed dose–response meta-regression found:

- detectable hypertrophy at roughly **4 “fractional” sets per muscle per week**;
- relatively high efficiency around **5–10 fractional sets**;
- continued average gains through higher volumes, but with progressively smaller returns and greater uncertainty;
- no clearly identified hypertrophy plateau within the well-studied range (Pelland et al., 2025).

For strength, the curve is more front-loaded. Very low volumes can produce meaningful gains, and additional sets show **much stronger diminishing returns**. One systematic review found that a single hard set of 6–12 repetitions, performed 2–3 times weekly, can produce significant but explicitly **suboptimal** squat and bench-press strength gains in resistance-trained men (Androulakis-Korakakis et al., 2020). The latest dose–response model placed most detectable strength benefit within roughly **1–4 fractional weekly sets of the tested movement**, although trained lifters may need more direct practice than mixed-population models suggest (Pelland et al., 2025).

The practical distinction is therefore:

> **Low volume is strongly defensible for obtaining a large share of possible strength and hypertrophy gains per unit of time. Higher volume is better supported when the aim is maximizing hypertrophy, provided it is recoverable and productive.**

For your exact schedule:

- **2 sets × 3 sessions = 6 direct weekly sets per exercise**, before counting overlap from other exercises. This sits comfortably in a productive lower-volume range for many muscles.
- **2 sets × 2 sessions = 4 direct weekly sets**, which approximately reaches the recent model’s minimum detectable hypertrophy dose.
- **1 set × 3 sessions = 3 direct weekly sets**, which is plausible but borderline for maximizing reliable growth unless other exercises also train that muscle.
- **1 set × 2 sessions = 2 direct weekly sets**, which can maintain or improve strength and may produce some hypertrophy, but is the least defensible version for an intermediate whose priority is continued muscle growth.

The regime becomes less appropriate when a target muscle receives too little total weekly work, sets are not actually close enough to failure, exercise selection leaves portions of a muscle undertrained, or progress has plateaued despite adequate nutrition and recovery.

## 1. The important unit is weekly work per muscle—not sets per exercise

“One or two sets per exercise” does not define a physiological dose unless the exercise menu and frequency are also known.

Two sets of bench press, overhead press, and dips in the same week do not equal only two sets for the triceps. Conversely, two sets of squats do not necessarily provide a complete dose for hamstrings, calves, or hip extensors. Recent meta-regression found that counting an indirect set as approximately **half a set** predicted outcomes better than counting it as either a full set or zero. The authors explicitly described this “fractional-set” method as a useful heuristic, not a biological constant (Pelland et al., 2025).

An illustrative full-body plan performed three times weekly might produce:

| Exercise | Sets/session | Direct weekly sets at 3×/week | Important indirect work |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Squat or leg press | 2 | 6 for quadriceps | gluteals, adductors |
| Romanian deadlift | 2 | 6 for hip extensors/hamstrings | spinal erectors |
| Bench press | 2 | 6 for chest | triceps, anterior deltoids |
| Row | 2 | 6 for upper back | elbow flexors |
| Overhead press | 2 | 6 for deltoids | triceps |
| Pulldown/pull-up | 2 | 6 for lats | elbow flexors |

That is not an extremely low-volume program at the muscle level. With overlap, some upper-body muscles could receive roughly 6 direct sets plus several fractional sets. By contrast, one set of one exercise per movement pattern twice weekly is genuinely minimal.

**Implication:** The question is not simply whether “two sets work.” It is whether each target muscle accumulates enough high-quality weekly work from direct and indirect exercises.

## 2. What the evidence says about the dose–response curve

### 2.1 Hypertrophy: one set works, but multiple sets usually produce more growth

Krieger’s early hypertrophy meta-analysis included 55 effects from 19 treatment groups in eight studies. Multiple sets produced a significantly larger hypertrophy effect than one set. Estimated effects rose from **0.24 for one set**, to **0.34 for 2–3 sets**, and **0.44 for 4–6 sets per exercise**. The 2–3 versus one-set and 4–6 versus one-set conventional comparisons were trends rather than conventionally significant, but both were significant in permutation testing. The difference between 2–3 and 4–6 sets was not significant. The author summarized the pooled multiple-set advantage as approximately **40% greater hypertrophy-related effect size** than one set (Krieger, 2010).

This does **not** prove that exactly two sets are 40% better than one. The evidence combined two and three sets, was based on only eight studies, and included both trained and untrained subjects. It does show that treating one set as generally equivalent to multiple sets is not supported.

A later meta-analysis shifted the focus from per-exercise sets to total weekly sets per muscle. It included 15 studies and 34 treatment groups. Each additional weekly set was associated with approximately **0.37 percentage points greater muscle gain**, and higher-volume conditions gained about **3.9 percentage points more** than lower-volume conditions on average. The categorical comparison of fewer than 5, 5–9, and 10 or more weekly sets showed a trend rather than a definitive category effect, but the continuous analysis supported a graded relationship (Schoenfeld et al., 2017).

The most recent peer-reviewed dose–response analysis substantially expanded this evidence. Pelland et al. (2025) analyzed 67 studies and 2,058 participants overall; the hypertrophy model included 35 studies, 220 effects, and 1,032 participants. Participants were mostly young men, and models adjusted for intervention duration and training status. The best-fitting hypertrophy curve was a **square-root function**:

- more weekly volume predicted more growth;
- returns diminished as volume rose;
- the model did not identify a clear plateau;
- uncertainty widened at high volumes because relatively few studies examined approximately 25 or more fractional weekly sets.

The authors’ practical “efficiency tiers” were:

| Fractional sets per muscle/week | Model-based interpretation |
|---:|---|
| 4 | Minimum dose sufficient to exceed the study’s smallest detectable hypertrophy effect |
| 5–10 | Higher-efficiency range |
| 11–18 | Intermediate efficiency |
| 19–29 | Lower efficiency |
| 30–42 | Lowest efficiency among modeled ranges |
| 43+ | Too little evidence; less growth also remains possible |

These are **not universal prescriptions**. They depend on the authors’ definition of a smallest detectable effect, pooled study designs, and the assumption that indirect sets count as one-half. The important conclusion is the curve’s shape: **the first several hard sets appear highly productive; further sets can add growth, but each additional increment requires progressively more work.**

### 2.2 Strength: a steeper early benefit and earlier taper

Krieger’s strength meta-regression included 92 effects from 30 treatment groups across 14 studies. Two to three sets per exercise produced significantly greater gains than one set, with an estimated **46% greater strength-gain effect**. Four to six sets were not significantly superior to either one or 2–3 sets, but that category was imprecise because it contained fewer observations (Krieger, 2009).

Wolfe et al. (2004), pooling 16 studies and 621 participants, found that multiple sets were particularly superior in **trained participants** and in longer programs. Their interpretation was that one set can work well during an initial short period in untrained people, but multiple sets become more useful as training status and desired progress increase.

Ralston et al. (2017) included nine studies, 61 treatment groups, and 223 male participants. They categorized weekly sets per exercise as low (≤5), medium (5–9), or high (≥10). High versus low volume produced a statistically significant but **small/trivial pooled advantage** for strength (effect-size difference 0.18), while medium versus low produced an advantage of 0.15. Thus, more volume helped on average, but the marginal difference was much smaller than the gain obtained by training at all.

The newest dose–response model reinforces this interpretation. Pelland et al. (2025) found a positive relationship between weekly volume and strength, but the best-fitting curve had **strong diminishing returns and a functional plateau**. Their model’s smallest detectable effect was exceeded at approximately one fractional weekly set, with additional detectable increments through approximately four sets. The authors cautioned that:

- their smallest detectable threshold of 3.96% may be larger than what some athletes consider meaningful;
- strength-test learning may inflate low-dose effects;
- well-trained powerlifters may require roughly 3–9 direct weekly sets rather than the 1–3 suggested by mixed-population data;
- most studies did not taper before post-testing, so accumulated fatigue could suppress high-volume performance.

Finally, a minimum-dose systematic review of six studies in resistance-trained men concluded that **one set of 6–12 repetitions at about 70–85% 1RM, performed 2–3 times per week with high effort for 8–12 weeks**, can significantly increase squat and bench-press 1RM. The authors explicitly labeled these gains **suboptimal** and noted weak evidence for deadlift, women, and highly trained strength athletes (Androulakis-Korakakis et al., 2020).

### 2.3 Direct answer: one set versus two sets versus more

The literature is much less precise than fitness discussions often imply:

1. **One set:** Clearly better than no training and capable of increasing strength and muscle size. It is especially time-efficient and can be sufficient for maintenance or early progress.
2. **Exactly two sets:** Surprisingly under-isolated in long-term hypertrophy trials. Most syntheses combine **2–3 sets**, so claims specific to two sets should be modest.
3. **Two to three sets:** Better supported than one set for average strength gains and probably for hypertrophy.
4. **Three to five or more sets:** Can produce more hypertrophy, particularly when this raises a muscle’s weekly total from a low dose to a moderate dose. It is not consistently superior for strength, and advantages become less predictable at already high weekly volumes.
5. **Very high volume:** May help some muscles or individuals, but evidence becomes sparse, short-term, and inconsistent. Above roughly 12–20 weekly sets, additional average hypertrophy appears smaller and muscle-specific; recovery cost rises.

The scientifically honest description is therefore **not** “two sets are all anyone needs.” It is:

> **Two hard sets often capture a large portion of the available benefit, especially when repeated 2–3 times weekly, but higher recoverable weekly volume generally has a higher probability of maximizing hypertrophy.**

## 3. The strongest counter-case: trained-subject studies favoring higher volume

The counter-evidence should not be minimized. Several studies in trained men show meaningful benefits from 3–5 or more sets.

### Schoenfeld et al. (2019): the most directly relevant counter-example

This trial is unusually close to your question:

- **Participants:** 34 healthy resistance-trained men completing the study; mean reported training experience approximately 4.4 years. Forty-five were initially allocated, leaving about 11–12 completers per group.
- **Duration:** 8 weeks.
- **Routine:** seven-exercise **full-body training three times weekly**.
- **Comparison:** 1, 3, or 5 sets per exercise per session.
- **Effort:** 8–12 repetitions to momentary concentric failure.
- **Outcomes:** squat and bench 1RM; ultrasound thickness at elbow flexors, elbow extensors, mid-thigh, and lateral thigh.

All groups increased strength and endurance, with no significant between-group differences. In contrast, hypertrophy significantly favored higher-volume conditions at the elbow flexors, mid-thigh, and lateral thigh; elbow extensor differences were not significant. The authors concluded that strength could be improved with three approximately 13-minute weekly sessions, but muscle growth followed a dose–response pattern.

This is strong evidence against claiming that one set per exercise is equally good for maximizing hypertrophy in trained lifters. It is not definitive because the trial was only eight weeks, had small groups and substantial attrition, used ultrasound at selected sites, and compared very large weekly differences. Based on the authors’ counting, weekly totals ranged from roughly 6–9 sets per muscle in the one-set group to 18–27 in the three-set group and 30–45 in the five-set group. Nevertheless, its full-body, three-day design makes it highly relevant.

### Marshall et al. (2011): higher volume improved trained-men squat strength

- **Participants:** 32 resistance-trained men.
- **Duration:** 6 weeks of volume-manipulated training, followed by a common 4-week peaking phase.
- **Routine:** squat at 80% 1RM, twice weekly.
- **Comparison:** 1, 4, or 8 sets to failure.

All groups improved, but the eight-set group became stronger faster and remained significantly stronger than the one-set group. The four-set group was not clearly different from either extreme. Importantly for a “functional” goal, early and peak rate of force development decreased in all groups under the high-intensity, to-failure prescription. This trial supports more volume for maximal squat strength but also illustrates that maximal-effort volume is not automatically optimal for power qualities.

### Brigatto et al. (2022): 32 weekly sets beat 16 for selected outcomes

- **Participants:** 27 resistance-trained men, only nine per group.
- **Duration:** 8 weeks.
- **Comparison:** 16, 24, or 32 weekly sets per muscle group.
- **Outcomes:** bench and squat 1RM; ultrasound thickness of biceps, triceps, and vastus lateralis.

All groups gained size and strength. Thirty-two sets produced larger lower-body 1RM and muscle-thickness changes than 16 sets, and greater triceps thickness gains. This favors a continuing dose response at high volumes, but precision is limited by the very small groups.

### Untrained studies also favor multiple sets—but generalize less well

Sooneste et al. (2013) used a within-person design in only eight **untrained** men over 12 weeks. Three preacher-curl sets produced greater MRI-measured elbow-flexor cross-sectional-area growth than one set (13.3% versus 8.0%); strength differences favored three sets numerically but did not reach conventional significance.

Rønnestad et al. (2007) studied 21 **untrained** men for 11 weeks. Three sets produced greater lower-body strength and thigh cross-sectional-area gains than one set, although upper-body outcomes did not differ. These studies strengthen the general multiple-set case, but novices are not a close match for an intermediate lifter.

## 4. Evidence that the higher-volume advantage is not unlimited or universal

Several trained-subject trials do not show a clean “more is always better” pattern.

| Study | Sample and duration | Compared dose | Main result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ostrowski et al. (1997) | 27 trained men; 10 weeks | 3, 6, or 12 weekly sets per muscle | All groups improved muscle size, strength, and upper-body power; no significant between-group differences. |
| Heaselgrave et al. (2019) | 49 trained men; 6 weeks | 9, 18, or 27 weekly biceps sets | No significant between-group differences. Muscle-thickness gains were 4.3%, 9.5%, and 5.4%, respectively, numerically favoring the middle dose. Frequency differed between the low and higher groups, complicating interpretation. |
| Aube et al. (2022) | 35 well-trained lifters; 8 weeks | 12, 18, or 24 lower-body weekly sets | No between-group hypertrophy differences. The 18-set group showed a strong trend toward better squat strength than 24 sets; several hypertrophy estimates numerically favored 12 sets. |
| Baz-Valle et al. (2022) | Systematic review; seven trained-male studies, six meta-analyzed | 12–20 versus >20 weekly sets | No significant advantage above 20 sets for quadriceps or biceps; an advantage appeared for triceps. |

These findings do not prove that lower volume is optimal. They show:

- substantial individual and muscle-specific variation;
- poorer marginal returns once volume is already moderate or high;
- a plausible recoverability ceiling;
- limited statistical power to detect modest differences;
- the importance of prior training volume.

Scarpelli et al. (2022) adds an important individualized point. In 16 trained participants using a within-person leg design over eight weeks, a weekly volume set at 1.2 times each participant’s previous volume outperformed a standardized 22-set prescription for vastus lateralis cross-sectional-area growth. The result suggests that **change from habitual volume**, not only an absolute universal set target, may affect adaptation.

## 5. Moderators that determine whether 1–2 sets are “enough”

### 5.1 Proximity to failure

Low-volume training depends heavily on set quality. If only one or two sets are performed, stopping many repetitions short of failure leaves fewer opportunities to recruit and fatigue high-threshold motor units.

The evidence does not show that literal failure is always required:

- Grgic et al. (2021) found no overall significant difference between failure and non-failure training for strength or hypertrophy across 15 studies. In trained participants, failure produced a small hypertrophy advantage, but the broader estimate was uncertain.
- A later proximity-to-failure meta-regression found that hypertrophy tended to improve as sets ended closer to failure, while strength showed no clear dose relationship with repetitions in reserve. However, repetitions in reserve were estimated from study descriptions, model fit was modest, and the authors cautioned against inferring an exact threshold (Robinson et al., 2024).

**Practical inference:** A lower-volume hypertrophy program should usually keep working sets within approximately **0–3 repetitions in reserve**, but not every set must reach absolute failure. For demanding free-weight compounds, retaining roughly 1–3 repetitions may preserve technique, power, and recovery. Safer machine or isolation exercises can be taken closer to failure when desired.

### 5.2 Frequency

When weekly volume is equated, frequency has little consistent independent effect on hypertrophy. A meta-analysis of 25 studies found no meaningful hypertrophy difference between lower and higher frequencies when volume was matched, including in trained participants (Schoenfeld et al., 2019).

Frequency still matters operationally:

- it determines whether “one or two sets” becomes two, three, four, or six weekly sets;
- it distributes fatigue and may preserve repetition quality;
- it provides more practice of strength movements;
- it makes a low per-session dose more viable.

Pelland et al. (2025) found negligible or inconsistent independent frequency effects for hypertrophy, but a positive, diminishing-return relationship for strength—probably partly from skill practice.

Full-body versus split routine is not itself a major hypertrophy or strength determinant when volume is equated. A 2024 meta-analysis included 14 studies and 392 participants and found similar strength and muscle-growth outcomes between split and full-body routines (Ramos-Campo et al., 2024). However, only four to six studies contributed to specific hypertrophy outcomes, so this is less conclusive than the headline sample suggests.

### 5.3 Total weekly volume per muscle

This is the central moderator. A two-set regime can be:

- **adequate:** two sets of an exercise three times weekly, plus useful overlap;
- **minimal:** one set twice weekly with no synergistic work;
- **moderate:** two sets across multiple exercises targeting the same muscle.

A reasonable lower-volume target for continued hypertrophy is approximately **4–8 or 10 high-quality fractional sets per muscle weekly**. This range is an evidence-informed interpretation, not a hard biological boundary. Muscles receiving only two or three weekly sets may still grow, but the probability and magnitude of growth are less secure for an intermediate.

### 5.4 Training status

Novices can improve with almost any coherent program, which makes novice evidence a weak basis for an intermediate’s optimum. Trained lifters:

- have smaller remaining adaptation margins;
- may need more volume to create overload;
- are better able to produce high force and therefore more fatigue per set;
- may need less volume for maintenance but more carefully individualized volume for further growth.

The evidence is mixed even in “trained” samples because training status is often self-reported as one or more years of lifting. It does not guarantee intermediate strength standards, consistent prior programming, or high technical competence.

### 5.5 Exercise selection

One or two sets only work if the chosen exercises actually train the intended musculature through productive ranges and can be progressively overloaded.

Multi-joint exercises are efficient, particularly for a generalist goal, but they do not expose every involved muscle equally. A meta-analysis found broadly similar whole-muscle hypertrophy from single- and multi-joint exercises, while acknowledging that combining them may better target muscular subdivisions (Rosa et al., 2022).

For a compact full-body program, exercise selection should normally cover:

- a knee-dominant pattern;
- a hip hinge or hip-extension pattern;
- horizontal and/or vertical pressing;
- horizontal and vertical pulling;
- direct work for muscles poorly covered by compounds when their growth matters;
- loaded carries, power work, locomotion, mobility, or conditioning according to the functional goal.

Changing exercises too frequently can also weaken strength tracking and overload. A low-set program benefits from stable, high-yield movements.

### 5.6 Load, progression, nutrition, and recovery

For hypertrophy, a wide loading range can work when sets are sufficiently hard. For maximal strength, higher loads and movement specificity matter more. Currier et al. (2023), a network meta-analysis of 178 strength studies and 119 hypertrophy studies, found that all tested prescriptions improved both outcomes versus no exercise. Higher-load, multiset, three-times-weekly training ranked highest for strength, while higher-load, multiset, twice-weekly training ranked highest for hypertrophy; many prescription estimates nevertheless overlapped.

No set prescription compensates for:

- failure to add repetitions or load over time;
- insufficient protein or energy availability;
- chronically poor sleep;
- pain-limited technique;
- excessive concurrent fatigue.

## 6. Application to a full-body 2–3×/week, generalist program

Your format is scientifically coherent. In fact, it has several advantages:

1. **Frequency converts a small session dose into a meaningful weekly dose.**
2. **Full-body training distributes practice and fatigue**, rather than concentrating many declining-quality sets in one session.
3. **Low-to-moderate volume preserves recovery capacity** for power, mobility, aerobic work, sport, and normal life.
4. **Adherence is likely higher** when sessions remain manageable.

The exact combination is not extensively tested. Schoenfeld et al. (2019) is directly relevant because it used full-body training three times weekly, but most volume studies isolate a few muscles or use bodybuilding-style routines. The full-body versus split literature generally equates volume and asks about organization, not whether one or two sets per exercise are enough. Trials also rarely measure hypertrophy alongside a broad battery of movement quality, power, conditioning, and real-world function. Evidence is therefore partly reconstructed from weekly-volume, frequency, effort, and fatigue studies rather than derived from many trials of your exact program and goal set.

### A defensible implementation

For hypertrophy plus functional fitness:

- perform **2 hard sets for most primary exercises**;
- use **2–3 weekly exposures**;
- keep most hypertrophy sets around **0–3 RIR**;
- avoid routine absolute failure on heavy squats, hinges, and presses if it harms technique, power, or subsequent training;
- include some heavy, specific practice if maximal strength matters;
- audit each muscle’s total direct plus fractional weekly sets;
- prioritize stable exercises that train substantial muscle mass through tolerable ranges;
- use small isolation additions for undercovered muscles rather than automatically raising every exercise to five sets.

This usually places a target muscle near 4–8+ weekly sets, depending on frequency and overlap. That is a defensible lower-volume growth dose.

### What higher-volume training would probably add

Moving from two to three or more sets per exercise would likely:

- increase expected hypertrophy, especially for muscles currently receiving fewer than approximately 5–10 weekly fractional sets;
- add relatively little strength for some movements once 2–6 quality weekly sets and adequate heavy practice are present;
- increase session duration and local/systemic fatigue;
- potentially reduce the quality of movement, power, or conditioning work;
- produce highly individual returns.

Thus, the relevant comparison is not “low volume works” versus “high volume works.” Both work. The question is whether the probable extra growth is worth the recovery and opportunity cost for your goals.

## 7. Limitations of the evidence

### Small samples

Key trained-subject trials often contain only **8–17 participants per condition**. Schoenfeld et al. (2019) had 34 completers across three groups; Brigatto et al. (2022) had nine per group; Sooneste et al. (2013) had only eight participants. Modest true differences can be missed, while unusually large results can be unstable.

### Short duration

The recent dose–response meta-analysis reported a mean intervention duration of approximately **10.4 ± 4.5 weeks** (Pelland et al., 2025). Most key hypertrophy trials last 6–12 weeks. This may not represent:

- years of progressive training;
- delayed fatigue from sustained high volume;
- the need to increase volume as adaptation slows;
- long-term adherence;
- whether low volume preserves progress better by avoiding burnout.

### Imperfect training-status labels

“Resistance-trained” often means self-reported regular lifting for one year. Samples may not represent strong, well-programmed intermediate lifters, and very few data address advanced women.

### Hypertrophy measurement

- **Ultrasound muscle thickness** is common and practical but samples selected sites rather than whole-muscle volume. Results can differ by measurement location.
- **MRI cross-sectional area or volume** is more anatomically specific but less common and still sensitive to protocol and site selection.
- **DEXA lean mass** is not a direct measure of skeletal-muscle hypertrophy and can be influenced by hydration, glycogen, organs, and other lean tissue.
- **Strength is not a hypertrophy proxy.** Neural learning, technique, leverages, and test familiarity can increase 1RM without proportional muscle growth.
- Edema is most concerning early in novel or damaging training; it is less likely to fully explain post-training changes in trained subjects, but hydration-related noise remains relevant, particularly in extreme-volume studies.

### Set counting is approximate

Counting every compound set as one full set for every synergist likely overstates dose; counting it as zero understates dose. The recent half-set method is empirically useful but still an assumption.

### Failure and effort are inconsistently defined

Pelland et al. (2025) noted that although most hypertrophy effects mentioned failure, only about 30% clearly defined momentary failure. Robinson et al. (2024) had to estimate RIR from study descriptions. “Hard set” therefore does not mean exactly the same thing across studies.

### High-volume survivorship and ecological validity

Research participants who complete very high-volume protocols are, by definition, those who tolerated them long enough to provide post-tests. Trials rarely quantify injury risk, psychological burnout, or the opportunity cost to sport and conditioning. Many protocols prioritize the measured muscle or lift rather than balanced whole-body training.

### Statistical and design limitations

- Meta-regressions are largely observational across study protocols even when they include randomized trials.
- Network meta-analysis relies partly on indirect comparisons.
- Multiple muscle sites and outcomes create multiplicity concerns.
- High-volume ranges contain fewer studies and wider uncertainty.
- No current model establishes a universal “maximum recoverable volume.”

## 8. Funding and conflict-of-interest audit

No obvious set-volume study in the core evidence base was identified as commercially sponsored to prove a low- or high-volume method. Disclosures are nevertheless relevant:

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2017):** reported no potential conflict of interest.
- **Ralston et al. (2017):** reported no funding and no relevant conflicts.
- **Pelland et al. (2025):** reported no funding. All five authors disclosed that they are coaches and writers in the fitness industry.
- **Robinson et al. (2024):** reported no funding. Robinson, Pelland, Remmert, and Zourdos disclosed coaching/writing roles in the fitness industry; other authors reported no relevant conflicts.
- **Currier et al. (2023):** reported no funding specifically for the review. Several authors held public scholarships. Senior author Stuart Phillips reported grants, contracts, fees, or other relationships with dairy, nutrition, and health companies and licensed patents, largely outside the submitted work; remaining authors reported no competing interests.
- Several older individual trials and older journal records provide less detailed or less accessible disclosure information than modern reports.

Commercial fitness work or nutrition-industry relationships do not by themselves invalidate a result, but they warrant transparency. More important here are the methodological limits: small samples, short duration, uncertain set counting, and sparse trained-lifter data.

## 9. Clear verdict

### Is the regime scientifically defensible?

**Yes.** A full-body program using 1–2 hard sets per exercise, 2–3 times weekly, is a scientifically defensible and time-efficient default for a healthy intermediate who wants hypertrophy while preserving movement and general fitness.

The strongest version of that conclusion applies when:

1. most major muscles receive at least roughly **4–8 high-quality direct/fractional weekly sets**;
2. working sets genuinely finish close to failure;
3. exercise selection covers the body rather than merely the favorite lifts;
4. loads or repetitions progress;
5. nutrition and recovery support growth;
6. the objective is **good or near-efficient progress**, not certainty of maximum possible hypertrophy.

### What the evidence does not justify

It does not justify saying:

- one or two sets are universally optimal;
- additional sets are “junk volume” after set two;
- failure makes weekly volume irrelevant;
- 1–2 sets will remain enough indefinitely for every muscle;
- low volume maximizes hypertrophy as reliably as moderate or high volume.

The evidence leans toward **more recoverable weekly volume producing more hypertrophy, up to a point that varies by person and muscle**, with diminishing returns. Strength reaches its diminishing-return region earlier than hypertrophy.

### Is one set as defensible as two?

Not equally:

- **Two sets performed three times weekly** is well supported as a productive lower-volume approach.
- **Two sets twice weekly** is defensible but sits near the lower boundary for continued hypertrophy if a muscle has no other work.
- **One set three times weekly** may work, especially with exercise overlap, but is more clearly a minimum-dose strategy.
- **One set twice weekly** is best framed as maintenance/minimal growth or a temporary low-volume phase—not a high-confidence hypertrophy prescription for an intermediate.

## 10. When to add volume

Do not add sets merely because a generic chart recommends more. Add them when outcome data and recovery capacity support doing so.

### Plateau signals

Consider increasing a target muscle’s volume when, for approximately **3–6 weeks**:

- repetitions and load do not progress across repeated exposures;
- technique and effort are stable;
- sets are already close enough to failure;
- body weight, protein intake, sleep, and stress are appropriate for the goal;
- the target muscle shows no change across standardized measurements, photos, or performance;
- other muscles or lifts are progressing, suggesting a local rather than systemic problem;
- soreness and fatigue are low, indicating room to recover from more work.

A hypertrophy plateau is difficult to establish over only one or two weeks because muscle growth is slow and measurement noise is large. Use at least several weeks of standardized observations.

### How to add volume

Add approximately **two weekly sets for the lagging muscle**, not two sets to every exercise in every session. For example:

- add one set on two of the week’s sessions;
- add a second exercise for one or two sets;
- distribute the added work to avoid a large per-session fatigue spike;
- retain the new dose for 4–8 weeks before judging it;
- reduce volume again if performance, joints, motivation, sleep, or movement work deteriorate.

This creates an evidence-based escalation ladder:

1. verify effort and progression;
2. verify weekly muscle coverage;
3. add a small amount of volume to the specific plateaued muscle or lift;
4. reassess;
5. reserve 3–5 sets per exercise for muscles that need and tolerate them, rather than treating that dose as mandatory everywhere.

---

## Bottom line

**Your current approach is not bro-science or merely a rationalization for doing less.** It is consistent with a large body of evidence showing that the early sets deliver a disproportionate share of strength and hypertrophy benefit.

But the equally important counterpoint is:

**If maximum hypertrophy were the sole goal, the evidence would favor more than the minimum—usually by raising total weekly sets per muscle, not necessarily by performing five sets of every exercise.**

For a generalist intermediate, **two hard sets per exercise, full body three times weekly** is arguably the most defensible form of the approach. It offers a good balance of weekly stimulus, practice frequency, fatigue, and time. Treat it as a default that earns continuation through progress—not as a permanent ideological ceiling on volume.
